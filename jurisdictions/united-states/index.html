Executive Summary

Transfer pricing in the United States concerns the pricing of transactions between related parties and the allocation of income, deductions, credits and allowances among controlled taxpayers. The system is built on Internal Revenue Code Section 482 and the Treasury Regulations under Section 482, whose purpose is to ensure that taxpayers clearly reflect income and do not avoid tax through non-arm’s-length pricing.

Unlike many OECD jurisdictions, the United States does not require a formal master file and local file structure. Instead, it uses contemporaneous documentation rules under Treasury Regulation Section 1.6662-6(d), primarily as a penalty-protection mechanism, and the documentation must generally exist by the time the return is filed and be produced to the IRS within 30 days of request.

The United States also applies country-by-country reporting through Form 8975 for US-parented multinational groups meeting the applicable revenue threshold and operates a formal APA program through the IRS as an alternative dispute resolution mechanism. In practice, US transfer pricing also interacts heavily with information reporting such as Forms 5471 and 5472.

In practical terms, the US regime is significant because it is federal, highly technical, economically analytical and strongly penalty-sensitive. It is especially relevant where a group has US entities, US branches, foreign-parented US corporations, intangible property, financing flows or large cross-border service and distribution arrangements.

Purpose

The purpose of the transfer pricing function is to ensure that related-party transactions connected to the United States reflect arm’s-length outcomes and are supported by sufficient evidence for federal tax compliance and dispute defence.

It exists to reduce Section 482 adjustment risk, support documentation integrity and create a coherent evidentiary basis for cross-border profit allocation involving US taxpayers, branches and related enterprises.

Primary Outcome

A defensible US transfer pricing position in which the relevant controlled transactions, selected method, economic support, contemporaneous documentation, information reporting and cross-border coordination are consistent with Section 482 and the Treasury Regulations.

Decision Tree

The decision tree simplifies scope and compliance questions that commonly determine the correct US transfer pricing approach.

  1. Identify whether the transaction is controlled and has US federal tax relevance.
  2. Determine whether Section 482 applies to the transaction or business arrangement.
  3. Assess whether sufficient functional analysis, agreements, accounting records and comparability support exist.
  4. Confirm that contemporaneous documentation will be complete by the time the federal income tax return is filed.
  5. Determine whether the group is subject to Form 8975 country-by-country reporting and related international information returns.
  6. Consider whether APA planning is advisable for significant, recurring or technically difficult arrangements.

FAQ

The FAQ section collects recurring threshold questions in a concise handbook format.

Does the United States Apply the Arm’s Length Principle to Controlled Transactions? Yes. The United States applies the arm’s length standard through Internal Revenue Code Section 482 and the Treasury Regulations under Section 482.
Does the United States Require Master File and Local File Documentation? No. The United States does not require an OECD-style master file and local file structure, but it uses contemporaneous documentation rules for penalty protection under Treasury Regulation Section 1.6662-6(d).
How Quickly Must Documentation Be Provided to the IRS After a Request? Contemporaneous documentation generally must be provided to the IRS within 30 days of a request.
Does the United States Require Country-by-Country Reporting? Yes. The United States requires country-by-country reporting on Form 8975 for qualifying US-parented multinational groups.
Does the United States Offer Advance Pricing Agreements? Yes. The IRS operates an advance pricing agreement program as an alternative dispute resolution mechanism for complex international transfer pricing matters.
Do Forms 5471 and 5472 Matter in Transfer Pricing Practice? Yes. These forms are important because they collect related-party information and must remain consistent with the taxpayer’s transfer pricing position.
