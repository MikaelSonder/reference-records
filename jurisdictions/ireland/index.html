Executive Summary

Transfer pricing in Ireland concerns the pricing of transactions between associated enterprises and the allocation of profits in both cross-border and certain domestic related-party arrangements. The Irish framework applies the arm’s length principle through Part 35A of the Taxes Consolidation Act 1997 and is construed in accordance with the OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines.

In operational terms, Ireland combines substantive transfer pricing rules with a threshold-based documentation framework. Larger taxpayers must prepare OECD-style documentation, with a Local File generally required from EUR 50 million group revenue and a Master File generally required from EUR 250 million group revenue.

Irish transfer pricing documentation must be prepared no later than the filing date for the corporation tax return for the chargeable period, which is usually nine months after the fiscal year end. If Irish Revenue issues a written request, the documentation must generally be produced within 30 days.

Ireland also provides bilateral APA procedures and applies country-by-country reporting from EUR 750 million group revenue. This makes the Irish system especially relevant for multinational groups with recurring intercompany transactions, financing, intellectual property, services and significant capital transactions.

Purpose

The purpose of the transfer pricing function is to ensure that related-party transactions connected to Ireland are priced consistently with the arm’s length principle and supported by the required documentation framework.

It exists to reduce adjustment risk, support audit defensibility and create a coherent evidentiary basis for cross-border profit allocation involving Irish taxpayers.

A defensible Irish transfer pricing position in which the relevant controlled transactions, pricing method, documentation set and cross-border coordination are consistent with the arm’s length principle and the actual conduct of the parties.

Decision Tree

The decision tree simplifies threshold questions that commonly determine the correct Irish transfer pricing approach.

  1. Identify whether the transaction is controlled and has Irish tax relevance.
  2. Assess whether group revenue is at least EUR 50 million and therefore triggers a Local File requirement.
  3. Assess whether group revenue is at least EUR 250 million and therefore triggers a Master File requirement.
  4. Assess whether group revenue is at least EUR 750 million and therefore triggers CbCR obligations.
  5. Review whether the arrangement involves non-trading domestic transactions or capital transactions that may now fall within the Irish rules.
  6. Consider bilateral APA strategy if the transaction is recurring, material or likely to create significant cross-border controversy risk.

Operating Constraints & Risks

Operating constraints identify the recurring friction points that affect transfer pricing execution in Ireland.

Threshold RiskThe taxpayer may misjudge whether the EUR 50 million, EUR 250 million or EUR 750 million group-revenue thresholds apply.
Documentation Timing RiskThe required documentation must be prepared by the corporation tax return filing date, not only after an audit begins.
Response Timing RiskThe taxpayer generally has only 30 days to provide documentation after a written request from Revenue.
Scope Expansion RiskIrish transfer pricing can apply to certain non-trading domestic transactions and capital transactions, which may be overlooked.
Penalty ExposureFailure to provide documentation within the required timeframe can trigger fixed penalties, including EUR 25,000 plus EUR 100 per day for continued failure where a Local File is required.

FAQ

The FAQ section collects recurring threshold questions in a concise handbook format.

Does Ireland Apply the Arm’s Length Principle to Controlled Transactions? Yes. Ireland applies the arm’s length principle through Part 35A of the Taxes Consolidation Act 1997, construed in accordance with the OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines.
When Is a Local File Required in Ireland? A Local File is generally required where the multinational group revenue is at least EUR 50 million.
When Is a Master File Required in Ireland? A Master File is generally required where the multinational group revenue is at least EUR 250 million.
