Executive Summary

Transfer pricing in the Czech Republic concerns the pricing of transactions between related parties and the allocation of profits in domestic and cross-border group arrangements. The Czech framework applies the arm’s length principle through the Income Taxes Act, especially Section 23(7), and is interpreted in line with OECD transfer pricing guidance.

In operational terms, the Czech Republic differs from some jurisdictions because there is no universal statutory obligation requiring all taxpayers to maintain transfer pricing documentation in a prescribed master file or local file form. However, the burden of proof rests on the taxpayer, and Guidance D-334 recommends OECD-style documentation that is widely prepared in practice by multinational groups.

The Czech system also includes a specific annex to the corporate income tax return that captures information about related-party transactions in relevant cases. In addition, country-by-country reporting applies to multinational groups with consolidated revenue of at least EUR 750 million.

The Czech Republic also offers advance pricing agreements and related ruling mechanisms. This makes the system particularly relevant for multinational groups operating manufacturing, shared services, distribution, financing and regional support structures connected to Central European supply chains.

Purpose

The purpose of the transfer pricing function is to ensure that related-party transactions connected to the Czech Republic are priced consistently with the arm’s length principle and supported by sufficient evidence for tax return reporting and audit review.

It exists to reduce adjustment risk, support audit defensibility and create a coherent evidentiary basis for cross-border profit allocation involving Czech taxpayers.

Primary Outcome

A defensible Czech transfer pricing position in which the relevant controlled transactions, pricing method, supporting documents, tax return annex disclosures and cross-border coordination are consistent with the arm’s length principle and the actual conduct of the parties.

Decision Tree

The decision tree simplifies scope and compliance questions that commonly determine the correct Czech transfer pricing approach.

  1. Identify whether the transaction is controlled and has Czech tax relevance.
  2. Determine whether the taxpayer must disclose related-party transactions in the corporate income tax return annex.
  3. Assess whether sufficient documentation exists to satisfy the taxpayer’s burden of proof during audit review.
  4. Consider whether OECD-style master file and local file documentation should be prepared under Guidance D-334.
  5. Determine whether the group reaches the EUR 750 million consolidated revenue threshold for CbCR.
  6. Consider whether the low value-adding intra-group services simplification may be relevant.
  7. Consider APA strategy if the transaction is recurring, material or likely to create significant cross-border controversy risk.

FAQ

The FAQ section collects recurring threshold questions in a concise handbook format.

Does the Czech Republic Apply the Arm’s Length Principle to Controlled Transactions? Yes. The Czech Republic applies the arm’s length principle through the Income Taxes Act, especially Section 23(7), interpreted in line with OECD guidance.
Is Transfer Pricing Documentation Mandatory in the Czech Republic? No general statutory obligation requires all taxpayers to maintain transfer pricing documentation in a fixed prescribed form, but the taxpayer bears the burden of proof and Guidance D-334 recommends OECD-style files.
Does the Czech Republic Require Country-by-Country Reporting? Yes. Country-by-country reporting applies to multinational groups with consolidated revenue of at least EUR 750 million.
Does the Czech Republic Offer Advance Pricing Agreements? Yes. The Czech Republic offers advance pricing agreements and related transfer pricing ruling mechanisms.
How Quickly Can Documentation Be Requested in a Czech Audit? In practice, documentation may be expected within 15 to 30 days, and in some audits effectively immediately on request.
Is There a Specific Related-Party Annex in the Czech Corporate Tax Return? Yes. A specific annex may be required to disclose related-party transaction information in relevant cases.
