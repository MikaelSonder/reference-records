Executive Summary

Transfer pricing in Canada concerns the pricing of cross-border transactions between non-arm’s-length parties and the allocation of profits in multinational group arrangements. Canada applies the arm’s length principle through section 247 of the Income Tax Act, and the Canada Revenue Agency treats transfer pricing as a key area of enforcement and review.

Canada does not use a formal statutory OECD master file and local file system as its core domestic model. Instead, it requires contemporaneous documentation under subsection 247(4) to demonstrate that the taxpayer made reasonable efforts to determine and use arm’s-length prices and terms.

The documentation due date is the filing due date of the relevant return, and the material must be provided to the CRA after written request within the applicable response window. Canada also operates an APA program through the CRA and applies country-by-country reporting for qualifying multinational groups.

In practical terms, the Canadian regime is highly relevant for groups with US-Canada flows, resource-sector structures, distribution arrangements, financing transactions, intellectual property licensing and inbound or outbound service models. The regime is especially sensitive because significant transfer pricing adjustments can trigger a 10 percent penalty once the statutory thresholds are exceeded and reasonable efforts are not established.

Purpose

The purpose of the transfer pricing function is to ensure that cross-border non-arm’s-length transactions connected to Canada reflect arm’s-length terms and are supported by sufficient evidence for tax compliance and dispute defence.

It exists to reduce section 247 adjustment risk, support documentation integrity and create a coherent evidentiary basis for cross-border profit allocation involving Canadian taxpayers and related offshore parties.

Primary Outcome

A defensible Canadian transfer pricing position in which the relevant cross-border controlled transactions, arm’s-length method support, contemporaneous documentation, reporting consistency and cross-border coordination are aligned with section 247 and CRA expectations regarding reasonable efforts.

FAQ

The FAQ section collects recurring threshold questions in a concise handbook format.

Does Canada Apply the Arm’s Length Principle to Controlled Cross-Border Transactions? Yes. Canada applies the arm’s length principle through section 247 of the Income Tax Act to cross-border non-arm’s-length transactions.
Does Canada Require OECD-Style Master File and Local File Documentation? No. Canada does not use a formal statutory master file and local file system, but it requires contemporaneous documentation to show reasonable efforts under subsection 247(4).
When Must Contemporaneous Documentation Be Prepared in Canada? The documentation due date is the filing due date of the taxpayer’s return for the relevant year.
How Quickly Must Documentation Be Provided to the CRA After a Written Request? For many existing references, the CRA requires the documentation within three months of a written request, while newer legislative changes apply to later taxation years.
Does Canada Offer Advance Pricing Agreements? Yes. Canada offers an APA program through the CRA as a cooperative process for resolving transfer pricing issues prospectively.
Can Transfer Pricing Penalties Apply in Canada? Yes. Significant transfer pricing adjustments can trigger a 10 percent penalty when the statutory thresholds are exceeded and reasonable efforts are not established.
