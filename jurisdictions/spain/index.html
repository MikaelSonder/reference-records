Executive Summary

Transfer pricing in Spain concerns the pricing of transactions between related parties and the allocation of profits in cross-border group arrangements. The Spanish framework applies the arm’s length principle through Article 18 of the Corporate Income Tax Law and follows OECD transfer pricing standards.

In operational terms, Spain combines substantive pricing rules with formal documentation and information reporting obligations. The system includes master file and local file concepts, a separate annual information return for certain related-party transactions through Form 232, and country-by-country reporting for large multinational groups.

Spanish transfer pricing compliance is highly threshold-driven. A master file is generally required for groups with net turnover above EUR 45 million, local file documentation becomes relevant where related-party transactions exceed EUR 250,000 in the relevant framework, and country-by-country reporting applies from EUR 750 million consolidated revenue.

Spain also offers advance pricing agreement procedures and tax audit review channels. This makes the Spanish system especially relevant for multinational groups with recurring intercompany transactions, tax haven exposure, high-value services, licensing or financing arrangements and potential documentation penalty risk.

Decision Tree

The decision tree simplifies threshold questions that commonly determine the correct Spanish transfer pricing approach.

  1. Identify whether the transaction is controlled and has Spanish tax relevance.
  2. Assess whether group net turnover exceeds EUR 45 million and triggers a master file requirement.
  3. Assess whether related-party transactions exceed EUR 250,000 in the relevant Spanish framework and therefore require specific local documentation.
  4. Determine whether Form 232 must be filed for related-party or tax haven transactions.
  5. Assess whether the multinational group reaches EUR 750 million consolidated revenue and triggers CbCR obligations.
  6. Consider APA strategy if the transaction is recurring, material or likely to create significant cross-border controversy risk.

FAQ

The FAQ section collects recurring threshold questions in a concise handbook format.

Does Spain Apply the Arm’s Length Principle to Controlled Transactions? Yes. Spain applies the arm’s length principle through Article 18 of Law 27/2014 on Corporate Income Tax.
Which Authority Administers Transfer Pricing Matters in Spain? The Agencia Estatal de Administración Tributaria, commonly referred to as AEAT or the Spanish Tax Agency, administers transfer pricing matters in Spain.
When Is a Master File Generally Required in Spain? A master file is generally required for groups with net turnover above EUR 45 million.
When Does Local File Documentation Become Relevant in Spain? Local file documentation is generally required when related-party transactions exceed EUR 250,000 per related entity and transaction type, subject to the Spanish regulatory framework.
Does Spain Require a Separate Annual Information Return? Yes. Spain requires Form 232 to report certain related-party transactions, tax haven transactions and certain other transfer pricing-related information.
What Is the Typical Audit Response Timing? If requested during a Spanish tax audit, transfer pricing documentation is typically expected within 10 business days.
