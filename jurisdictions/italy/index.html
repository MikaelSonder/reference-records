Executive Summary

Transfer pricing in Italy concerns the pricing of transactions between related parties and the allocation of profits in cross-border group arrangements. The Italian framework applies the arm’s length principle through Article 110, paragraph 7 of the Income Tax Code and follows OECD transfer pricing standards in domestic practice.

In operational terms, Italy combines substantive pricing rules with an optional but highly relevant documentation regime. Although transfer pricing documentation is not generally mandatory, taxpayers that prepare compliant documentation can obtain penalty protection in case of transfer pricing adjustments.

Italian documentation is structured around two principal documents: the Master File and the Country File, the latter functioning as the Italian local entity file. The documentation must meet formal requirements, including language rules, electronic signature and time stamp requirements, and must generally be submitted within 20 days if requested by the tax authorities.

Italy also provides advance tax agreements for enterprises with international activities and applies country-by-country reporting for large multinational groups. This makes the Italian system especially relevant for multinational groups with recurring intercompany transactions, manufacturing, distribution, licensing, financing or permanent establishment issues.

Purpose

The purpose of the transfer pricing function is to ensure that related-party transactions connected to Italy are priced consistently with the arm’s length principle and, where chosen, supported by compliant documentation that can provide penalty protection.

It exists to reduce adjustment risk, support audit defensibility and create a coherent evidentiary basis for cross-border profit allocation involving Italian taxpayers or Italian permanent establishments.

Primary Outcome

A defensible Italian transfer pricing position in which the relevant controlled transactions, pricing method, documentation set, formal compliance steps and cross-border coordination are consistent with the arm’s length principle and the actual conduct of the parties.

Decision Tree

The decision tree simplifies threshold and compliance questions that commonly determine the correct Italian transfer pricing approach.

  1. Identify whether the transaction is controlled and has Italian tax relevance.
  2. Determine whether the taxpayer wants access to penalty protection through suitable documentation.
  3. Prepare the Master File and Country File in the required structure if penalty protection is sought.
  4. Confirm that the Country File is in Italian and that the Master File is in Italian or English.
  5. Ensure electronic signature, time stamp and annual tax return notice are completed on time.
  6. Assess whether the multinational group reaches EUR 750 million consolidated revenue and triggers country-by-country reporting obligations.
  7. Consider advance tax agreement strategy if the transaction is recurring, material or likely to create significant cross-border controversy risk.

FAQ

The FAQ section collects recurring threshold questions in a concise handbook format.

Does Italy Apply the Arm’s Length Principle to Controlled Transactions? Yes. Italy applies the arm’s length principle through Article 110, paragraph 7 of the Italian Income Tax Code.
Is Transfer Pricing Documentation Mandatory in Italy? No. Transfer pricing documentation is generally optional in Italy, but properly prepared documentation may provide penalty protection.
Which Documents Are Used in the Italian Documentation Regime? Italy uses a Master File and a Country File, which functions as the local Italian entity file.
In Which Language Must Italian Transfer Pricing Documentation Be Prepared? The Country File must be drafted in Italian, while the Master File may be prepared in Italian or English.
How Quickly Must Documentation Be Submitted on Request? The documentation must generally be submitted within 20 days after a request from the Italian tax authorities.
When Does Country-by-Country Reporting Apply in Italy? Country-by-country reporting applies for Italian ultimate parent entities of multinational groups with consolidated revenue of at least EUR 750 million.
