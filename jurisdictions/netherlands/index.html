Executive Summary

Transfer pricing in the Netherlands concerns the pricing of transactions between associated enterprises and, where relevant, dealings involving permanent establishments. The Dutch framework applies the arm’s length principle through domestic legislation and is closely aligned with the OECD transfer pricing guidelines.

In practice, the Netherlands combines a broad arm’s length rule with a layered documentation framework. All taxpayers must be able to document and substantiate their intercompany transactions, while larger multinational groups are subject to a more formal master file, local file and country-by-country reporting structure.

The Dutch regime follows the most appropriate method approach rather than a strict legal hierarchy of methods. It also accepts a relatively practical international perspective, including the use of foreign or pan-European comparables where this is justified by the facts and comparability conditions.

The Netherlands also offers advance pricing agreement procedures and broader dispute prevention tools. This makes the Dutch system especially important for multinational groups using the Netherlands for holding, financing, licensing, distribution or regional coordination functions.

Purpose

The purpose of the transfer pricing function is to ensure that related-party transactions connected to the Netherlands are priced consistently with the arm’s length principle and supported by appropriate documentation.

It exists to reduce tax risk, support defensible cross-border structuring and create a coherent evidentiary basis for multinational arrangements involving Dutch entities or permanent establishments.

A defensible Dutch transfer pricing position in which the relevant controlled transactions, pricing method, documentation framework and cross-border alignment are consistent with the arm’s length principle and the actual conduct of the parties.

Process Flow

The process flow explains how Dutch transfer pricing work usually progresses from transaction identification to documentation readiness and possible certainty procedures. It matters because the Netherlands combines a universal documentation duty with threshold-based formal file obligations for larger groups.

1. Transaction MappingIdentify the controlled transactions connected to the Netherlands, including financing, services, licensing, distribution and permanent establishment dealings.
2. Documentation Threshold ReviewAssess whether the general documentation duty applies alone or whether the EUR 50 million and EUR 750 million thresholds trigger formal master file, local file or CbCR requirements.
3. Arm’s Length AnalysisReview whether pricing and terms correspond to those that would have been agreed between independent parties.
4. Method SelectionSelect the most appropriate transfer pricing method based on the facts, functions, risks and available comparables.
5. Documentation BuildPrepare and maintain the necessary documentation in Dutch or English, including general support or formal OECD-style files where required.
6. Tax Return ReadinessEnsure the documentation framework is in place by the corporate income tax filing deadline where the formal file requirements apply.
7. APA or Review RouteIf needed, move into certainty procedures, tax authority review or dispute prevention channels.
Typical OutputsGeneral TP documentation, master file, local file, CbCR support, benchmarking analyses, intercompany transaction support and APA materials.
Decision Tree

The decision tree simplifies threshold questions that commonly determine the correct Dutch transfer pricing approach.

  1. Identify whether the transaction is between associated enterprises and has Dutch tax relevance.
  2. Confirm the general documentation duty under Article 8b.
  3. Assess whether consolidated group revenue reaches EUR 50 million, triggering master file and local file obligations.
  4. Assess whether consolidated group revenue reaches EUR 750 million, triggering country-by-country reporting obligations.
  5. Determine the most appropriate method and prepare support in Dutch or English.
  6. Consider APA strategy if the transaction is recurring, material or sensitive from a cross-border risk perspective.

FAQ

The FAQ section collects recurring threshold questions in a concise handbook format.

Does the Netherlands Apply the Arm’s Length Principle to Controlled Transactions? Yes. The Netherlands applies the arm’s length principle through Article 8b of the Dutch Corporate Income Tax Act 1969.
Must Dutch Taxpayers Document All Intercompany Transactions? Yes. Article 8b paragraph 3 of the Dutch Corporate Income Tax Act requires taxpayers to document and substantiate all intercompany transactions.
When Are Master File and Local File Obligations Triggered? A Dutch entity in a multinational group with consolidated revenue of at least EUR 50 million must maintain a master file and local file.
Does the Netherlands Offer APA Procedures? Yes. The Dutch system provides advance pricing agreement procedures as part of its dispute prevention and certainty framework.
In Which Language May Dutch Documentation Be Kept? Dutch transfer pricing documentation may be kept in Dutch or English.
Does the Netherlands Use a Fixed Hierarchy of Transfer Pricing Methods? No. The Dutch regime follows the most appropriate method approach rather than a strict legal hierarchy of methods.
